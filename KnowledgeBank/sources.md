# Credible Reference Sources for JLPT N5

This document lists authoritative and widely trusted sources used as references for JLPT N5 grammar patterns, kanji (moji), and vocabulary (goi).

## Official Sources

- **Japan Foundation & Japan Educational Exchanges and Services (JEES)** - [https://www.jlpt.jp](https://www.jlpt.jp)
  - The official JLPT administering bodies. Their site provides the official test specifications, sample questions, and the "JLPT Can-do Self-Evaluation List" for each level. **Note (verified 2026-04):** Starting December 2025, JLPT score reports include CEFR reference levels alongside the N1-N5 result.

- **Japan Foundation - JF Standard for Japanese Language Education** - [https://www.jfstandard.jpf.go.jp](https://www.jfstandard.jpf.go.jp)
  - Official framework developed by the Japan Foundation since 2010, based on CEFR. Defines language proficiency benchmarks (A1-C2 mapping) and is the basis for the Japan Foundation's worldwide JF Language Courses.

## Foundational Reference Lists (Pre-2010 Official Lists)

- **Original JLPT Test Content Specifications (しゅつだいきじゅん / *shutsudai kijun*)** - Published by the Japan Foundation (1994/2002 editions)
  - The original officially published vocabulary and kanji lists for the old Levels 3 and 4, which form the historical basis of the modern N5 list. Although the post-2010 JLPT no longer publishes an official list, this remains the academic baseline used by virtually all textbooks and study sites.

## Major Textbooks (Industry Standards)

- **Minna no Nihongo Shokyū I** - 3A Corporation
  - One of the most widely adopted beginner Japanese textbooks worldwide. Lessons 1-25 align closely with N5 grammar and vocabulary.

- **Genki I: An Integrated Course in Elementary Japanese** - The Japan Times Publishing
  - Standard textbook used at universities globally. Chapters 1-12 cover N5 content comprehensively. **Note:** the current edition is the 3rd Edition (Genki 3 / だい 3 はん); 2nd Edition is still widely circulated. Vol. 1 is officially aligned to JLPT N5 and CEFR A1 by the publisher. Official site: [genki3.japantimes.co.jp](https://genki3.japantimes.co.jp/en/).

- **Try! N5 (アスクしゅっぱん / Ask Publishing imprint)** - Ask Publishing
  - JLPT-specific prep series produced by Japanese publishers; trusted by examinees and teachers.

- **Nihongo Sō-Matome N5 / Nihongo Challenge N5** - ASK Publishing / Unicom
  - Series authored by experienced JLPT instructors; often used by self-learners and language schools.

## Academic & Educational Platforms

- **The Japan Foundation - Marugoto** - [https://marugoto.jpf.go.jp](https://marugoto.jpf.go.jp) (canonical; the older `www.marugoto.org` 301-redirects here)
  - Official Japan Foundation course platform aligned with JF Standard / CEFR. A1 (Starter) + A2 (Elementary 1 & 2) overlap with N5; both Katsudoo (activities) and Rikai (comprehension) tracks are published as books and downloadable PDFs.

- **Tofugu (Kanji & Grammar Articles)** - [https://www.tofugu.com](https://www.tofugu.com)
  - Editorially reviewed long-form articles on Japanese grammar and kanji; widely cited by learners and educators.

- **Imabi** - [https://imabi.org](https://imabi.org)
  - Comprehensive grammar reference covering ~450 lessons across ten proficiency tiers (beginner through classical Japanese). Authored and maintained by a long-time educator (the author explicitly notes they are not a native speaker, but content is developed with native-speaker review). The site is undergoing an ongoing remodel through 2026.

## Established Learner References

- **Jisho.org** - [https://jisho.org](https://jisho.org)
  - Free online Japanese-English dictionary built on JMdict/EDICT and KANJIDIC databases (curated by the Electronic Dictionary Research and Development Group). Provides JLPT level tags per entry.

- **JMdict / EDICT / KANJIDIC / KANJIDIC2** - Electronic Dictionary Research and Development Group (EDRDG) - [https://www.edrdg.org](https://www.edrdg.org)
  - Long-running open-data Japanese dictionary projects established at Monash University in 2000. Maintains JMdict / EDICT (general bilingual dictionary), KANJIDIC / KANJIDIC2 (kanji data), JMnedict / ENAMDICT (proper names), KRADFILE / RADKFILE (kanji-radical mappings), and the WWWJDIC server. The underlying source for most learner dictionary apps including Jisho.org.

- **WaniKani** - [https://www.wanikani.com](https://www.wanikani.com)
  - Structured kanji and vocabulary SRS platform. Curriculum covers 2,000+ kanji and 6,000+ vocabulary items across 60 internal levels, ordered by frequency-informed mnemonics. Readings and meanings are reviewed by Japanese-language educators. Note: WaniKani does not organize content by JLPT level - N5 content is spread across roughly Levels 1-10.

- **Bunpro** - [https://bunpro.jp](https://bunpro.jp)
  - Grammar-focused SRS platform organized by JLPT level; references Tae Kim, Genki, Tobira, and other established sources.

## Reference Grammar Works

- **A Dictionary of Basic Japanese Grammar** - Seiichi Makino & Michio Tsutsui (The Japan Times)
  - The canonical academic reference for beginner-to-intermediate Japanese grammar; cited by virtually all serious learners and teachers.

- **Tae Kim's Guide to Learning Japanese** - [https://guidetojapanese.org](https://guidetojapanese.org)
  - Free online grammar guide widely recommended for its clear, linguistically grounded explanations.

## Why These Sources Are Reliable

- **Official authority** - JLPT site, Japan Foundation, and JEES define the test itself.
- **Editorial review** - Major textbooks (Minna, Genki, Try!) are produced by established publishers with native-speaker editors.
- **Academic grounding** - Makino & Tsutsui's dictionaries and EDRDG's dictionary projects are peer-recognized references.
- **Consensus** - Cross-referencing these sources yields the standard JLPT N5 content set used across the industry.

## Usage Policy (Pedagogical Lens)

This project's content is curated with classroom and exam outcomes in mind, not theoretical completeness:

- **Exam realism first** - The list prioritizes *what is realistically tested at N5*, not every form, reading, or borderline item that exists in the language.
- **Conflict-resolution rule** - When sources disagree on whether an item is N5 or N4, **Minna no Nihongo + Genki overlap and frequency of appearance** is treated as the authoritative tiebreaker.
- **No silent N4 creep** - Borderline items are kept only when they appear in mainstream N5 prep textbooks, and are tagged as Extended/Borderline rather than mixed into the Core list.

## Exam Scope vs Reference Depth

Some references on this list (e.g., Imabi, Makino & Tsutsui, Tae Kim) go significantly deeper than the JLPT N5 syllabus. Their inclusion is intentional but bounded:

> Advanced explanatory references are used for **accuracy of interpretation** of N5 items (e.g., precise nuance of は vs が, transitive/intransitive pairs), **not to expand scope** beyond N5.

In short: deep references inform the *quality* of N5 explanations; they do not push the *quantity* of N5 content upward.
